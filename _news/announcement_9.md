#### About the Paper

The paper documents the pedagogical design and first-semester outcomes of _The Digital Eagles Nest_, a digital humanities course at NCCU in which undergraduate students author an architectural archive of their own campus. Six students worked in teams to scan buildings, record oral histories from alumni and facilities staff, and build 3D models — beginning with the James E. Shepard Administration Building — producing a working archive that accumulates across course cohorts.

The argument of the paper is pedagogical as much as archival: when students decide what to record, they decide how their institution will be remembered. The course shifts students from observers of architectural history to makers of it, treating documentation itself as an interpretive act. Assessment asks one question above all others — what did you add to the archive, and does it hold up?

The paper also addresses the particular stakes of this work at an HBCU. NCCU's campus carries a material record of Black education in the United States — renovation layers, sealed corridors, informal gathering spaces carved from rooms nobody officially claimed. Oral histories from alumni and staff complicate what the floor plans say, and students scanning familiar spaces are not merely recording a history but extending it.

CAA is the leading scholarly organization for the visual arts and art history in higher education.
