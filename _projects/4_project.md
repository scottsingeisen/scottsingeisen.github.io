This research examines how lithography and fine art printmaking shaped the public reception of major American architectural projects — an underexplored intersection of architectural history and art history. Before photography dominated architectural media, printmaking was the primary means by which buildings reached broad audiences, influencing how works were understood, celebrated, or critiqued.

The project investigates specific cases where printed images diverged from built reality, and how those representations established lasting perceptions of canonical works. A National Endowment for the Humanities Award for Faculty at HBCUs has been submitted in support of this research ($37,500, under review).
